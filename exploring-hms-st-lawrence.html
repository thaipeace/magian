THE USE OF
PHOTOGRAPHY

Photogrammetry is a technique that uses overlapping digital images taken with a camera, and processes these with a computer to recreate the scene as an accurate 3-dimensional object. Photogrammetry has become a powerful tool in archaeology, particularly in mapping underwater wrecks. Nearly two thousand photos were taken to create this model of the HMS St. Lawrence and many hours of processing to give us this model.

SHIP PLANS

These are the ship plans drawn by the British Admiralty to record the building of the HMS St. Lawrence after the war. The ship was actually built first, and the plans later. Compared to ocean going warships, Great Lakes ships tended to be shallow-drafted with sharper V-shaped hulls. Ocean-going vessels needed this additional cargo space to carry enough provisions for long voyages, but Great Lakes ships were more like floating platforms for as many guns as they could carry.

A NEW PURPOSE

In 1833, the hull of the St. Lawrence was towed out of Navy Bay by the Rideau, a new sidewheel steamer. The ship was towed across town, to the site of Robert Drummondâ€™s brewery. Upon reaching its destination, a storm blew up, running the hull aground. Here, Drummond set up the hull as a cordwood dock, where steamships would be able to pick up supplies of wood to use as fuel. It is not known for how long the hull of the St. Lawrence was used in this capacity, but it was never moved from this spot, and its wreck remains at this location to this day.

POST-WAR DECAY

When the War of 1812 ended, the the Treaty of Ghent, The HMS St. Lawrence and the other ships in the Lake Ontario squadron were laid up and kept in reserve at the Royal Naval Dockyards. All the guns were removed and the the ships were demasted, leaving only their hulks. The hulk of the St. Lawrence was a landmark on Kingstonâ€™s waterfront for many years, slowly falling into disrepair until it was auctioned off in 1832.

A MODEL OF THE WRECK

This 3D image shows the remains of HMS St. Lawrence today. It was created using photogrammetry, a technique using many hundreds of photographs processed by a sophisticated computer program to recreate the scene in three dimensions. Although only a 30-metre section of the lower hull remains, photogrammetry allows marine archeologists to match the remains to the original ship plans.

A GREAT WARSHIP

This is a painting by an unknown artist showing the launch of the HMS St. Lawrence on September 10, 1814. The HMS St. Lawrence was the largest sailing war vessel ever launched on the Great Lakes. Its gun deck was 191 feet (58 metres) long, and the ship was carried 102 guns. No American ship dared confront the HMS Ontario through the remainder of the war, giving the British complete control of Lake Ontario.

SOLD AT AUCTION

On January 18, 1832, the hulls of the St. Lawrence and three other ships were put up for public auction at the Royal Naval Dockyards in Kingston. The St. Lawrence was sold to Robert Drummond, a local brewer who intended to use the hull as a dock, for Â£25. The ship was towed out of Navy Bay in 1833.
