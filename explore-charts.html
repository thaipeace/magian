Monarch

Freight steamer

November 6, 1906

The Monarch foundered when it crashed headlong into a solid rock wall on the tip of Isle Royale during a snowstorm on its last trip of the season. All men but one managed to make it to shore, where they survived for four days while awaiting rescue.

SS Kamloops

Freight

December 7, 1927

The SS Kamloops was built specifically for travel within the Great Lakes, designed to fit in the tight confines of the Welland Canal. As part of the Canada Steamship Lines fleet, it delivered cargo between ports on the Great Lakes and St. Lawrence River. It foundered in a winter storm, and remained undiscovered for 50 years.

Hesper

Bulk freight steamer

May 4, 1905

Launched in 1890, the Hesper was a wooden-hulled steamer primarily used to transport iron ore and grain. It was caught in a spring storm and foundered on a reef. No lives were lost, but the wreck was considered a total loss.

Madeira

Schooner-barge

November 28, 1905

Three major storms occurred on the Great Lakes in 1905: on September 2nd, October 20th, and November 28th. Ten ships sank in these storms on Lakes Superior, Huron, and Erie, with many more damaged. The Madeira was being towed by a steamship, but was cut loose in the storm and crashed into a cliff.

Carl D. Bradley

Self-unloading freighter

November 18, 1958

The Carl D. Bradley was headed for winter dry dock maintenance when it was caught in a violent storm. The ship broke in two, and all but two of its crew perished. It is the second-longest shipwreck in the Great Lakes, at 639 feet (195 metres), after the SS Edmund Fitzgerald.

Alvin Clark

Schooner

June 29, 1864

The Alvin Clark was raised intact in 1969, and set up as a tourist attraction in a nearby town. However, no preservation measures were put in place, and the ship and its contents rapidly deteriorated once exposed to air. Its remains were demolished in 1994. The story of the Alvin Clark illustrates the importance of preservation and conservation of shipwrecks.

Frank O’Connor

Wooden steamer

October 2, 1919

The Frank O’Connor was one of the largest wooden ships to ever travel on the Great Lakes, at 301 feet (92 metres) long. The hull was strengthened with iron and steel strapping, and the ship was powered by a modern steam engine. It caught fire on its way to Milwaukee, WI, and though the ship sank, all crew members survived.

Waubuno

Sidewheel paddle steamer

November 22, 1879

The Waubuno was carrying a heavy load of freight and passengers when it was caught in a storm and foundered. Approximately 30 people were on board, all of whom perished. The wreck, located in shallow water in eastern Georgian Bay, is a popular dive site.

Daniel J. Morrell

Bulk freighter

November 29, 1966

A freighter that had been used on the Great Lakes for 60 years, the Daniel J. Morrell got caught in a violent storm and broke in two pieces. As the bow sank, the stern continued steaming before sinking five miles away. Only one person in the 29-man crew survived.

Charles S. Price

Cargo freighter

November 9, 1913

The Charles S. Price was one of eight ships lost with all hands in lower Lake Huron in the infamous Great Storm of 1913. This storm, which ravaged Lakes Superior, Michigan, Huron, and Erie, lasted three days, from November 8-10. In total, 67 ships were lost or damaged, and at least 248 lives lost.

HMS Ontario

22-gun 2-masted brig-sloop

October 31, 1780

During the Revolutionary War, HMS Ontario was the largest British warship to sail on the Great Lakes. It was launched just five months before it sank, and was used to ferry troops, supplies, and prisoners. It never saw battle.

Milan

Schooner

October 13, 1849

Built in 1845, the Milan sprung a leak while on its way from Oswego, NY to Cleveland, OH with a cargo of 1000 barrels of salt. The crew tried unsuccessfully to pump out the inflowing water and make it to shore, but eventually had to abandon ship. Nine crew members and a Newfoundland dog were on board the Milan, all of whom were safely rescued by a passing ship.

Three Brothers

Dagger-board schooner

November 12, 1833

Dagger-board schooners were used in the Great Lakes in the early 19th century. A board extending through the hull acted as a keel in rough waters, but could be removed to bring the ship close to shore. Three Brothers was the first fully-functional ship of this type ever found.

Roberval

Steamship

September 25, 1916

The Roberval made frequent trips between Ottawa, ON and Oswego, NY in the early 20th century to exchange coal and lumber. The large load of spruce it was carrying when it sank saved four people, as they managed to stay afloat on a raft of lumber until help arrived.

James B. Colgate

Whaleback steamer

October 20, 1916

Whaleback steamers have hulls that curve above the waterline, designed to reduce the impact of waves on the sides of the ship. On October 20, 1916, a massive storm swept over Lake Erie, sinking four ships including the James B. Colgate and killing 49 people. It later became known as Black Friday.

G P Griffith

Passenger steamship

June 17, 1850

The G P Griffith was carrying 326 passengers when the ship caught fire in the night and struck a sandbar while attempting to get to shore. Though it was only half a mile from shore, most passengers could not swim and subsequently drowned. Death toll estimates range from 250 to 295 people, making this the third-greatest loss of life in any Great Lakes shipping disaster.

Admiral

Tugboat

December 2, 1942

The Admiral was towing a tanker barge, the Cleveco, from Toledo to Cleveland when it encountered a winter storm. The Admiral foundered and the Cleveco was left without propulsion. Rescue ships were unable to locate the Cleveco; it is believed to have sunk while awaiting assistance.

Argo

Barge

October 20, 1937

The Argo was carrying crude oil and benzol when it foundered in a storm. In 2015, 78 years after it sank, it was found to be leaking hazardous chemicals into Lake Erie. A cleanup response was completed, removing all of the remaining chemicals from the wreck.

Wolfe Islander II

Ferry

September 21, 1985

After almost 30 years ferrying vehicles between Kingston and Wolfe Island, the Wolfe Islander II was scuttled to create a dive site, the first such use of a commercial vessel in the Great Lakes. Since then, it has become the most-visited shipwreck in Lake Ontario.

HMS Prince Regent

Frigate

Mid-1830s

The HMS Prince Regent was a British warship that patrolled Lake Ontario during the War of 1812. It was built in Kingston at the Royal Naval Dockyards, where it was launched in April 1814. It had little use after the war, and was put up for sale in the early 1830s, but no buyer was found and it eventually sank in Deadman Bay.

HMS St. Lawrence

Brig

Unknown; abandoned 19th century

The HMS St. Lawrence is considered the largest sailing war vessel ever to sail the Great Lakes. Launched in September 1814 for use in the War of 1812, the ship carried 102 guns and could support almost 1,000 men. The St. Lawrence never saw battle.

Barge

Unknown

The KPH wreck, named for its proximity to the Kingston Psychiatric Hospital, is a well-known dive site in Kingston. It is believed that the wreck may be the remains of the Waterlily, a steam barge that was constructed in 1870 and operated until at least 1916.

Horace Taber

Schooner

November 27, 1922

Constructed in St. Clair, MI in 1867, the Horace Taber became a Canadian vessel in 1911. It was headed to Kingston with a load of coal in late 1922 when it got caught in a snow storm and ran ashore. Though the ship was a complete wreck, the cargo was salvaged.

Effie Mae

Scuba charter boat

October 17, 1993

From ca. 1980 to 1992, the Effie Mae was used as a live-aboard charter ship for scuba diving. In the early-1990s, the ship’s owners were moving out of Ontario and donated the vessel to Preserve Our Wrecks Kingston. The ship was scuttled next to the schooner-barge Aloha in approximately 17 metres of water, and remains a popular dive site.

Aloha

Schooner barge

October 28, 1917

Built in 1888, the Aloha was being towed to Kingston with a load of 925 tons of coal when it was caught in a storm and foundered. The tug towing the ship, the Chamberlain, managed to save some of the crew, though the captain of the Aloha drowned.

Comet

Sidewheel steamer

May 14, 1861

Built in 1848, the Comet foundered and was raised and repaired several times in its life. It struck a shoal in its first few trips and sank, then in 1851 its boiler exploded, tearing a hole in its hull and sinking again. After several more accidents, it was caught in a storm and struck another ship. Taking on water, passengers and crew abandoned ship, leaving the Comet to sink for the last time.

George T. Davie

Barge

April 18, 1945

Built in 1898, the George T. Davie spent 47 years hauling coal and grain on Lake Ontario and the St. Lawrence River. On a routine trip delivering coal from Oswego, NY to Kingston, the ship began leaking and sank. All crew members were picked up by the tug Salvage Prince, which had been towing the ship.

LEARN

The bottom of the Great Lakes is the resting place for thousands of ships that have sunk over the last 400 years.

These shipwrecks can be found across all five Great Lakes, including many in eastern Lake Ontario near Kingston.

Each shipwreck is like a time capsule of history from the time the ship was built and sunk. Most of us will never get to visit these wrecks, but luckily many professional and recreational divers have made the journey and brought back photos.
